      Abstract—Single-photon detectors are crucial elements of prospective quantum devices and techniques such as quantum communication systems, quantum computers, quantum radars, astrophysical detectors, etc. The detection of individual photons in microwave range is a much larger challenge as compared to that of in optics due to the quanta energy difference of 4 to 5 orders of magnitude which requires a quite different approach. So “artificial atoms” with discrete energy spectrum such as superconducting qubits are used here exploiting resonance transitions between their quantum states. A scheme incorporating a flux qubit with controllable spectrum as a single photon counter for 3-12 GHz range and weak continuous readout circuit based on rf superconducting quantum interferometer (SQUID) with UHF pumping is proposed and analyzed. High SQUID pumping frequency (about 500 MHz) along with using cooled microwatt HEMT preamplifier provides the measurement frequency band of up to 30 MHz while substantially diminishing the back-action effect.


      Keywords—single-photon counter; microwave; Josephson junction; flux qubit; SQUID; tunable frequency

      1. INTRODUCTION

        The progress of experimental physics and nanotechnology, the accelerating development of information technology from digital to quantum, especially in quantum information processing gave a strong push to enhance the e< ciency of photon detectors to work at the quantum limit in various frequency ranges, starting from the optical range [1,2].

        Single-photon detectors are inevitable units in implementing quantum cryptography for quantum key distribution [3], in experimental tests of the foundations of quantum mechanics such as Bell tests [4] and in building optical quantum computers [5] among several other applications.

        The technique of detecting single quanta in photonics is currently well developed up to commercially available solutions, in the framework of processing quantum information [6,7] and the development of hybrid quantum Internet [8].

        Currently, significant experimental and theoretical activity is observed in studies of single-photon counters based on artificial atoms in the gigahertz frequency range [9- 15]. This interest was mainly stimulated by the possibility of constructing quantum information communication channels by analogy with experiments in the optical range [16-19].

        Moreover, there are applications of quantum sensing that are naturally belong to the microwave frequency range. This is clearly exemplified by the case of quantum illumination [20] with its remarkable robustness to background noise, approaching about 1000 thermal quanta per one photon received at a few GHz. In quantum illumination, the aim is to detect the presence of a low-reflectivity object in the presence of very bright thermal noise. This is accomplished by probing the target with a few entangled photons per mode, in a stealthy non-invasive fashion which is impossible to reproduce with classical means.

        However, a decrease in the energy of the microwave photon by 4–5 orders of magnitude as compared to the optical one significantly complicates the task of constructing the counter that requires searching for other physical principles, designing new element base, and cooling the device to temperatures as low as 10–30 mK.

        Also the remarkable achievements in the development of the elemental base for constructing quantum computers based on superconducting qubits [21,22] gave rise to some hope for the possibility of their practical implementation and attracted close attention to the development of quantum informatics using microwave electromagnetic fields. Despite the obvious difficulties mentioned above, single photon counters at 3.8 GHz and 10.2 GHz frequencies were proposed and demonstrated recently [10,12].

        The basic elements for constructing frequency-selective counters are superconducting nonlinear Josephson structures with discrete energy spectra, which are analogues of single atoms. The existence of discrete energy levels was demonstrated by observations of macroscopic resonant tunneling in a superconducting interferometer [23] and resonant absorption of microwave power in electromagnetic field-induced decays of metastable current states in a Josephson junction [24].         The photon counter based on a single Josephson junction encounters limitations in its performance since they work at final voltages and dissipate electric power. Even after the counter enters the superconducting state, additional time is required to achieve an equilibrium state at a refrigerator temperature of 10–30 mK. This time is difficult to make less than 1 ms, and it determines the time interval between registration of individual photons. In addition, the suppression of the “electromagnetic temperature” of circuits galvanically connected to the Josephson junction requires careful broadband filtering [23] to reduce the “dark” readings. Nevertheless, despite the difficulties noted, the Josephson-junction photon counters have good prospects due to simplicity of creating a chain of such detectors in this approach.

        The noted disadvantages of the photon counter based on a single contact can be eliminated by including it in a superconducting loop. The absence of galvanic coupling in this case provides better isolation from the environment, the control and signal registration circuits, making the system in a sense “more” quantum. For brevity, we will call this topology “rf SQUID”, implying that we are talking about a superconducting quantum interferometer with a geometric inductance less than the so-called fluctuation inductance, which is determined by thermal fluctuations and limits the coherent behavior of the loop.

        A very interesting version of such a broadband device based on an artificial atom as a three-level -system was considered in [12]. Physical phenomena occurring in this single-photon counter were analyzed in detail in [13,14]. Along with registration scheme which included a Josephson parametric oscillator coupled with the -atom and tracked the change in the phase of the oscillations, the counter showed high photon efficiency of 0.67 and speed (measurement bandwidth) of 16 MHz.

        It was shown in [15] that good performance (up to 15– 20 MHz) can be obtained in a counter based on a single- contact interferometer (rf SQUID) with discrete energy levels and a signal recording channel that exploits highly nonlinear curvature of quantum superposition levels of a qutrit as a function of external magnetic flux. The advantage of such a scheme for monitoring a quantum system is minimal back action from the side of measuring apparatus on the discrete levels of the interferometer (counter). However, a further increase in the speed of the measuring channel based on the qutrit will be limited by the superposition state settling time which is associated with a slow tunneling process.

The SQUID loop is made as a gradiometer to suppress external magnetic noise. Strictly speaking, the potential of this double-SQUID is two dimensional. With the system prepared in the upper well, a photon-assisted tunneling process may be expected that results from absorption of a single MW photon with frequency MW by the TFQ SPC.

        This transition occurs in two steps. First, the flux qubit jumps from the ground level (n=6) up to an excited one (n=7) such that E7 —E6 = hωMW, and then tunnels to the lower (right in Fig. 2) well as indicated by the arrow. Since the barrier between the wells at the excited level is much less than that at the ground one, the tunneling rate to the other well is greatly enhanced due to the photon absorption. The tunneling to the lower well and further relaxation to lower levels results in a change of the magnetic flux (of some tenth of 0, see Fig. 2) in the TFC SPC loop which has to be measured as the photon count ‘click’.

        Both temperature and dissipation (characterized by resistance R which should account for emission losses, effect of the measuring circuit and Josephson junction normal resistance) tend to cause a breakdown of the simple quantum picture present above. To observe quantum effects, the following inequalities must be satisfied: ħω0>>kBT, for no incoherent mixing of quantum states, and R>>(L/C)1/2, for energy level width to be smaller than the level separation. The width (smearing) of the energy levels is determined by the temperature and should be an order of magnitude smaller than the distance between neighboring resonant levels. Thus, to register photons with a frequency of about several GHz, the counter temperature should not exceed 10-20 mK. And for 10 GHz photons, as long as ħω0>>kBT (10 GHz and 30 mK correspondingly), we expect that finite temperature is not the drawback factor in this picture.         The damping, determined by R, broadens and shifts the energy of the quantized levels. This broadening of the energy levels is proportional to 1/Q, where Q=RCω0 is the quality factor for the TFQ SPC and is closely related to the spontaneous decay between energy levels within a well. The energy level shifts are proportional to 1/Q2 and negligible at high value Q>100.

        In order to use resonant photon absorption, which causes transitions between levels and a change in magnetic flux in the loop, to detect a microwave photon, the levels themselves must be well defined. The losses in high quality Nb/AlxOy/Nb tunnel junctions should be negligible at T<50 mK. We believe the dominant source of the damping in TFQ SPC system is due to other lossy circuits, e. g. the SQUID magnetometer, and various filters and coils (signal gate, gate 1 and gate 2), inductively coupled to the counter loop through the mutual inductances. The noise flux generated in the TFQ can then be related to a current generated by an equivalent resistance across the Josephson junction. The damping resistance from the gate 1, gate2 and the magnetometer with small mutual inductance of about 5 pH is then estimated to be ~9 kΩ whose thermal noise should be accounted during the energy spectrum engineering to not place the upper exited level too close to the barrier top.

      3. RF SQUID READOUT

        Magnetic coupling between the TFQ and the SQUID magnetometer can be achieved by placing them close to each other and varying coupling coefficient by changing the inter- spacing. The near-optimal value of the coupling coefficient is about 1/200. The TFQ and the SQUID loops share therefore a small part of common magnetic flux via their mutual inductance of ~3–6 pH. Then the TFQ flux change of

        ~0.5 Φ0 (see Fig. 2) generates a signal of ~2 mΦ0 the SQUID magnetometer.

        With shot noise accounted, the self energy sensitivity of modern dc SQUIDs at an ultra-low temperature (~30 mK) is about ~(5–10).10-34 J/Hz. A major drawback of a read-out for counters based on a dc SQUID is its “back action” due to Josephson generation and noise from the shunt resistance. The same is true for the rf SQUID in hysteretic mode due to energy dissipation.

        Theoretical analysis and experimental results show that an rf SQUID in a non-hysteretic adiabatic regime can work as an ideal parametric up-converter making therefore a negligible contribution to the total noise of the device. The sensitivity of the rf SQUID in the non-hysteretic regime rises with increasing pump frequency ω. In addition, an increase in ω allows us to expand the bandwidth and performance of the measuring channel. One can show that in this mode of operation (which is also known as the “dispersive” or “inductive” mode) the sensitivity of the SQUID mounted at the millikelvin temperature stage is limited by the quantum noise of the cooled amplifier with temperature Ta rather than the SQUID itself.

        In this regime, rf SQUIDs can be tuned to produce an “infinite” value of the transfer function [27]. To give an example, for rf SQUIDs with a non-hysteretic phase diagram operating at about 10 GHz and T=4.2 K, the energy

        sensitivity as good as 2.5.10–33 J/Hz has been reported in the literature [28]. This is about two orders of magnitude better than for any hysteretic rf SQUID.

        The sensitivity of the signal registration channel and its back action at the quantum states of the counter (dark counter rate, DCR) directly depend on the characteristics of the cooled amplifier. To comply to the weak continuous measurements (WCM) method, we developed a UHF amplifier with ultra-low power consumption based on HEMT working in so-called unsaturated dc regime [29]. Conceptual one-stage 10 dB-gain amplifier showed sub- microwatt level of the power consumption (0.95 μW at frequency of 0.5 GHz) when cooled down to 300 mK. Moreover, the brightness temperature of the amplifier input (which is a measure of how the amplifier affects the detector in a broad-band frequency range) in this regime is comparable to the physical temperature of the amplifier itself [30] thus providing minimum back action due to back Plank's irradiation onto the TFQ. For example, first amp stage consuming 1 μW is to be placed at 30 mK-level of a dilution refrigerator, while the other two at ~300 mK and

        ~1.5 K correspondingly. In this case, the noise temperature of the qubit-to-amplifier transmission line and the spectral density of Planck radiation emitted by elements of the measuring circuit will be determined thermodynamically by the physical temperature of 30 mK.

        In contrast to the scenario of dressed-state qubit [12-14] and in the framework of WCM philosophy, we consider the minimal influence of electromagnetic fields of the SQUID pumping onto the counter tuned to detect photons with a frequency of ~10 GHz. Firstly, we select the rf SQUID pumping frequency to be much less than the frequency of the measured photon. For example, when registering photons with a frequency of 10 GHz, the pump frequency is selected in the UHF range of 500–800 MHz, outside the resonance peaks of multiphoton transitions to reduce DCR. Secondly, the rf SQUID pumping is limited to the domain of small amplitudes (a << Φ0).

      4. CONCLUSION

We proposed and analyzed the schematic design of a single microwave photon counter that includes a tunable flux qubit and a signal registration channel based on an rf SQUID in the non-hysteretic regime with an ultra-low power consumption UHF amplifier based on HEMT operating in unsaturated dc regime. The analysis shows that the very high sensitivity of the SQUID measuring channel to a change in magnetic flux provides the frequency band of the device of up to 25 MHz with a minimal back action. We have shown that in this scenario the TFQ counter, not yet fully optimized; demonstrates the potential for detecting single microwave photon. We plan experimental verification of the proposed idea within the framework of an international project.
